> Across the Greek Islands, tourism pressure is straining local environments, but fragmented data keeps planners, policymakers and communities from responding at the scale required.

### Lessons for Policymakers

> Data Commons in tourism offers a structured, evidence-based approach to managing growth while ensuring sustainability and equity. By democratizing access to critical datasets and fostering collaboration across local governments, community organizations, and private entities, it equips mayors and urban policymakers with robust tools for evidence-based decision-making, focusing on social equity and climate resilience. 

Tourism-related climate and resource pressures across Mediterranean island destinations are intensifying faster than fragmented governance structures can respond. Decisions on carrying capacity, infrastructure investment, and adaptation continue to be made on incomplete or siloed data, held separately by municipalities, tourism operators, utilities, and community organisations. This leaves mayors and urban planners without the integrated evidence needed to act at the scale and speed the climate transition requires.
A Data Commons offers a governance mechanism, not just a technical infrastructure, to close this gap. By establishing a shared framework for the equitable re-use of climate, tourism, and urban datasets, cities and island municipalities can move from reactive crisis management to anticipatory, evidence-based planning grounded in local conditions.
Three actions for city policymakers and mayors:

1. Establish a data stewardship function within municipal government. This means a designated role responsible for brokering responsible data partnerships across public, private, and community actors, rather than treating data sharing as an ad hoc IT task.
2. Co-design carrying capacity thresholds with affected communities, using the Data Commons to make trade-offs visible: between visitor revenue and water stress, between short-term rental yields and housing access, between tourism employment and environmental degradation.
3. Embed data re-use agreements in regulatory and licensing frameworks for tourism operators, short-term rental platforms, and infrastructure providers, ensuring that the data needed to manage public goods is consistently available to those governing them.

The shift required is from data as a by-product of tourism to data as shared civic infrastructure for climate-resilient, equitable urban futures.
